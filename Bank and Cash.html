Overview

The big picture of the Bank & Cash Cycle — what it covers and how questions are structured

📌 From your revision slides: Same 3 question types as Inventory — (1) Design a Perfect System, (2) WRC — Weaknesses / Consequences / Recommendations, (3) Control Objectives using V · A · C. The perfect system principle: Documents drive the process.

What does the Bank & Cash Cycle cover?

  • Cash Receipts — cash register, debit/credit card, EFT direct deposits
  • Cash Payments — EFT payments, direct deposits to suppliers, petty cash
  • Monitoring — daily cash-up reconciliation, cash count, bank reconciliation, petty cash reconciliation
  • Links to: Revenue & Receipts (receiving money) and Acquisitions & Payments (paying suppliers)

Key Controls — The Perfect System at a Glance

  • Segregation of Duties (SoD) — cashier ≠ chief cashier ≠ accountant ≠ person who deposits
  • Two people count cash at shift end — joint responsibility
  • Daily cash-up reconciliation — physical cash vs theoretical (float + sales – card payments)
  • Pre-numbered documents — receipts, petty cash slips, debit card slips → sequence checks
  • Surprise cash counts — by someone independent of the cash register function
  • Bank reconciliation — monthly, by independent person, reviewed by senior
  • Sealed bags + safe — custody of cash between shift end and deposit
  • Cancel supporting documents after payment — prevents double payment

V · A · C for Bank & Cash

  • Validity Receipts are real (not fictitious), authorised, in correct period; payments are for valid liabilities
  • Accuracy Correct amounts recorded; debit/credit card slips matched; bank recon items correct
  • Completeness All receipts banked; all receipts and payments recorded; none omitted

WCGW — Cash Receipts Risks

  • Theft of receipts V
  • Incorrect amount received A
  • Not all cash receipts banked C
  • Fictitious receipt recorded V
  • Amount recorded incorrectly A
  • Amount recorded in wrong period V
  • Not all receipts recorded C

WCGW — Cash Payments Risks

  • Fictitious payments made V
  • Payments not authorised V
  • Incorrect amount paid A
  • Payments recorded at wrong amounts A
  • Double payments made C
  • Petty cash stolen V
  • Payments not recorded C

Cash Receipts — Perfect System

Know the controls for each receipt type: cash register, debit/credit card, and EFT direct deposits

  • Segregation of duties — chief cashier issues the cash float; cashier takes custody of it
  • Cash float is handed over in a sealed bag
  • Cash register is locked and only accessible by the chief cashier; cashier gains access only after a transaction is captured
  • Cash register must be mounted/secured to prevent removal from premises
  • Each cashier allocated a dedicated cash register
  • Cashier counts the cash float and signs a float register acknowledging custody
  • Chief cashier signs the float register as proof of issuing the float
  • A notice on premises reminds customers to request a receipt
  • Security cameras and guards on premises
  • Both the cashier and the chief cashier count the cash in the register together
  • Cashier prepares a daily cash-up reconciliation — see the Reconciliations tab for the format
  • Any differences (surplus or shortage) must be investigated and followed up immediately
  • Chief cashier reviews and approves the reconciliation — checks accuracy, logic, and follows up differences
  • Both cashier and chief cashier sign the reconciliation as evidence
  • Cash is placed in a sealed money bag and handed to the chief cashier
  • Both parties sign a register to acknowledge transfer of custody of the sealed bag
  • An independent person (e.g. financial manager) performs surprise cash counts
  • Independent person checks the number sequence of all debit card slips for the day
  • Independent person reviews the date sequence of daily cash-up reconciliations to identify missing days
  • A sign must be clearly visible requesting customers to request a receipt
  • The cash register must be linked to an authorised financial institution to verify online:
    • Whether the customer's card has not expired
    • Whether the customer has sufficient funds
  • The machine must automatically generate a pre-printed, pre-numbered, multi-copied debit/credit card slip with:
    • Name of cardholder, date of transaction, total amount paid
  • Copies distributed: one to cashier (for cash-up & sequence checks), one to customer, one to accounting department
  • Cashier checks: expiry date, signature on back of card vs slip, customer ID vs name on card
  • If transaction does not go through: cashier makes a copy of front and back of card to resubmit
  • Debit card slips are excluded from physical cash in the daily cash-up reconciliation (they are non-cash)
  • EFTs and direct deposits arrive directly in the bank — no physical document triggers the receipt
  • Cash book clerk records receipts in the cash receipts journal (CRJ) using bank statements as evidence
  • An independent person (e.g. junior accountant) reviews the cash receipts journal by comparing entries to supporting documentation and signs as proof
  • An independent person reviews the date sequence of filed bank statements to ensure none are missing
  • An independent person follows up on any unmatched invoices — invoices that have not been matched to deposits may indicate fictitious invoicing or uncollected debts
  • Unidentified deposits are recorded in a suspense account — must be reconciled to the updated list of unidentified deposits by an independent person
  • The bank reconciliation must be reviewed by a senior accountant who signs as evidence
  • All receipts are posted to the general ledger and debtors ledger; monthly debtors reconciliation is prepared
💡 Key principle: Cash must be deposited daily and preferably by a person independent of the cashier. The person who receives cash should NOT be the same person who records it, deposits it, or approves the reconciliation.

Cash Payments — Perfect System

EFT payments to suppliers, petty cash controls, and custody of cash before deposit

  • Trigger: Supplier's monthly statement / invoice with payment advice — already matched to a GRN or other evidence of a valid liability
  • Payment clerk A prepares the EFT payment requisition and payment schedule
  • Payment clerk B (independent) reviews the payment schedule and supporting documentation
  • Senior management approves and authorises the EFT payment — reviews all supporting documentation before authorising
  • After authorisation, supporting documents must be cancelled (stamped "PAID") to prevent double payment
  • Payment clerk A loads the EFT on internet banking
  • Payment requires one or two authorisation codes as per the entity's authorisation policy
  • Payment clerk A records the payment in the cash payments journal (CPJ)
  • Payment clerk B (independent) reviews the CPJ entry
  • All entries posted to the general ledger and creditors ledger
  • Monthly bank reconciliation and creditors reconciliation (creditors control vs creditors ledger) performed

Prior to initiation — Policy must define:

  • Initial amount (petty cash advance) and how often it is topped up
  • Maximum amount that can be issued for one transaction
  • The types of transactions petty cash can be used for
  • One person only is responsible for keeping petty cash in a safe and issuing money — only this person has the key
  • Money issued from petty cash must be kept separate from other cash

Process:

  • Employee completes a pre-numbered petty cash slip stating: nature of expense, date, amount requested
  • Employee attaches the supporting invoice to the petty cash slip
  • Line manager authorises the petty cash slip
  • Petty cash supervisor issues the cash and cancels the supporting invoice after payment
  • Petty cash slips are filed in sequence
  • An independent person performs a sequence check on slips and matches them to invoices
  • Petty cash supervisor prepares a petty cash reconciliation — see Reconciliations tab
  • Independent person reviews the petty cash reconciliation and signs as proof
  • Surprise petty cash counts must be performed by an independent person
  • At end of shift, cashier places all notes and coins into a sealed money bag
  • Cashier signs a register acknowledging transfer of custody to the chief cashier
  • Chief cashier signs the register as proof of receiving the sealed bag
  • Sealed bag is locked in a safe — key accessible only by a senior/independent person (e.g. financial manager)
  • Cash is deposited on a daily basis, preferably by a person independent of the cash register function
  • A deposit slip is prepared (date, amount in cash, total amount) — copy retained
  • The keys to the overnight safe must not be kept on the premises

Weaknesses, Consequences & Recommendations

From S5.20, S5.22, S5.24 — the most common exam question type

📌 Remember: Each row = 3 marks (1 weakness + 1 consequence + 1 recommendation). Identify the gap compared to the perfect system. Consequence = what goes wrong if this is not done. Recommendation = add/fix exactly what is missing.

Cashier and barista do NOT count the cash float when they receive it at the start of shift Too little or too much cash may be provided — errors undetected; responsibility cannot be allocated Both the cashier and the person issuing the float must count it together upon receipt

No reconciliation of cash receipts with sales for the day (no daily cash-up reconciliation) OR no sequence check on receipts Not all amounts received are put in the cash drawer and deposited (theft); errors in receipts not identified Prepare a daily cash-up reconciliation: cash advance + sales per till − card sales = physical cash. Joe (or manager) must sign and the accountant must review and sign
Insufficient segregation of duties — the same person (assistant) collects cash drawers, counts cash, AND deposits at the bank Fraud: assistant can adjust the amount he receives before depositing to conceal theft; errors in financial statements if shortages concealed Sufficient SoD must apply — an independent person (e.g. Joe) must handle the depositing of cash, separate from the person who counts it

Fraud Considerations

Cash is the highest-risk asset — know these fraud types for the exam

🔄 Lapping / Rolling of Cash

  • What it is: A cashier steals cash paid by Debtor A, then covers the shortfall using a subsequent payment from Debtor B — the theft keeps rolling forward
  • Higher risk when: cash is received from debtors, poor SoD between cashier and recording function, lack of independent review
  • Why it works: The time gap between the transaction date and the date it is recorded is exploited to hide the theft
  • How to prevent: Strict SoD — person who receives cash must NOT record receipts; surprise cash counts; independent review of CRJ

💸 Theft of Cash Receipts

  • Cashier pockets cash and does not issue a receipt or ring up the sale
  • Prevented by: Notices requesting receipts, cash register slips, daily cash-up reconciliation, surprise counts, CCTV cameras

🏦 Fictitious Deposits

  • Where customers can pay via direct deposit/EFT — a customer submits a fake proof of payment and receives goods/services without actually paying
  • Prevented by: Only releasing goods once the deposit is confirmed on the actual bank statement (not just proof of payment), reconciliation of deposits to bank statement

🔑 Key Anti-Fraud Controls to Remember

  • Segregation of duties is the single most important control against cash fraud
  • Surprise cash counts deter ongoing theft — employees don't know when a count will happen
  • Cancel supporting documents after payment to prevent double payment fraud
  • Bank reconciliation prepared and reviewed by independent people — detects manipulation of cash book
  • CCTV cameras and security guards — physical deterrent and evidence
