Overview

What you need to know about this cycle & how questions are asked

📌 Exam Tip from your revision slides: Questions come in 3 types: (1) Design a Perfect System, (2) Weaknesses / Consequences / Recommendations — compared to the perfect system, and (3) Control Objectives — identify or formulate using V · A · C

What is the Inventory & Production Cycle?

  • Focuses on managing, safeguarding, and accounting for inventory
  • For manufacturers: also controls the production process and movement of inventory (raw materials → WIP → finished goods)
  • Links with the Purchases & Payments cycle (receiving goods) and Sales & Receipts cycle (dispatching goods)

The Perfect System Principle

  • Documents drive the process — every movement needs a document
  • Segregation of Duties (SoD) — the person who requests ≠ person who issues ≠ person who records
  • Pre-numbered documents — allows sequence checks, detects missing/duplicates
  • Two people at key points (receiving, counting)
  • Independent review — someone checks the work of the person who prepared it
  • Physical vs Records reconciliation — perpetual inventory must be reconciled to physical counts

V · A · C — The 3 Control Objectives

  • Validity Authorised, occurred, during correct period, supported by documents
  • Accuracy Correct amounts, quantities, prices, calculations, classification
  • Completeness All transactions recorded, none omitted, recorded timeously

S · C · R · R · A · M — Design Question Checklist

  • Segregation of duties
  • Custody controls (access to assets)
  • Review / authorisation
  • Recording (journals, ledgers)
  • Arithmetical & accounting controls (sequence checks, reconciliations)
  • Management controls (surprise counts, overall oversight)

Control Objectives

How to formulate them — always start with "To ensure that…" — no people, no actions, no documents

🔑 Key Rule: Control objectives must be written in full sentences. Start with "To ensure that…" — no marks for bullet lists without formulation. Do NOT name people, specific actions, or documents in the objective itself.
  • To ensure that all recorded production transactions are valid and are supported by supporting documentation.
  • To ensure that production transactions and adjustments are authorised in accordance with company policy.
  • To ensure that recorded inventory movements actually occurred during the period.
  • To ensure that access to warehouses is limited to authorised staff (prevent theft/unauthorised use).
  • To ensure that credit adjustments for goods returned are only given if the customer was previously invoiced for them.
  • To ensure that all credit adjustments for goods returned are authorised in terms of company policy.
  • To ensure that production transactions are recorded accurately at the correct quantity and amount.
  • To ensure that the correct quantity and type of goods returned by a customer are recorded.
  • To ensure that the correct price of goods returned is recorded.
  • To ensure that calculations of credit adjustments are correct.
  • To ensure that inventory transactions are classified correctly (e.g. between WIP and raw materials).
  • To ensure that all valid production transactions which occurred are recorded and nothing is left out.
  • To ensure that all inventory movements are recorded in the correct financial period.
  • To ensure that no transactions are omitted from the perpetual inventory records.
⚠️ Common Exam Mistake: Students write "The storeman must compare documents" — that's a control, not an objective. An objective says what you want to achieve, not how.

Documents & Transaction Flow

Know every document, its purpose, its details, who prepares it, and how many copies

1. Production Schedule

Initiated by factory manager. Authorises what products to manufacture and allocates work to technicians.

↓

2. Raw Material Requisition

Prepared by manufacture clerk in production. Requests raw materials from the warehouse. Signed by Factory Manager.

↓

3. Raw Material Transfer Note (Issue Note)

Prepared by raw material storeman. Authorised by store manager. 3 copies: warehouse / production / accounting.

↓

4. Finished Goods Transfer Note

Prepared by manufacture clerk. Authorised by factory manager. Transfers FG from production to FG warehouse. 3 copies.

↓

5. Goods Received Note (GRN)

Prepared by 2 receipts clerks when receiving from supplier. Compared to supplier delivery note + purchase order. Authorised by warehouse manager.

↓

6. Perpetual Inventory Records

Continuously updated for every movement. Controlled by someone independent of storemen. Reconciled to general ledger and physical count.

↓

What must be on every document? (½ mark each)

  • Date of the transaction
  • Pre-number (sequential number for sequence checks)
  • Description of goods (quantity + type)
  • Quantity of goods
  • Name of supplier / receiving department
  • Signature of preparer + authoriser

Why 3 copies?

  • Copy 1 → Originating department (proof of request/issue)
  • Copy 2 → Receiving department (proof of receipt / for comparison)
  • Copy 3 → Accounting department (to update inventory/perpetual records)

Perpetual Inventory System — Advantages (S3.13)

  • Inventory records are automatically updated for every movement
  • Inventory can be monitored continuously — complete records always available
  • Inventory can be reordered in time when stock is low
  • Comparison between actual physical inventory and theoretical records can be done at any time
  • Makes inventory counts faster and easier to compare with records

Weaknesses, Consequences & Recommendations

The most common exam question type — compare to the perfect system. Each weakness must link to its consequence and recommendation.

📌 Exam Tip: Each row is 3 marks: 1 for weakness · 1 for consequence · 1 for recommendation. Consequences often relate to fraud, theft, errors, or misstatement. Recommendations mirror the weakness — if something is missing, recommend adding it.

Production & Inventory Movement Weaknesses (from S3.13 & S3.26)

WeaknessConsequenceRecommendation
No formal production planning / production schedule Work is allocated informally — production may be uncontrolled, goods manufactured without authorisation Factory manager must compile a formal pre-approved production schedule and allocate work to each technician
Technicians have direct access to the component warehouse — select items themselves Raw materials can be stolen or incorrectly issued — incorrect quantities used in production Appoint a foreman in warehouse; technicians must not have access; all issues via a requisition form
No documents to regulate the request or distribution of components (no raw material requisition) Incorrect quantities can be issued; responsibility for fraud/error cannot be allocated Technician completes a pre-numbered raw material requisition — signed by them and approved by production manager before issue
Raw material issue note is NOT pre-numbered AND no sequence check is performed Missing or duplicate issue notes may not be detected — incorrect classification between WIP and raw materials; out-of-stock situations possible Pre-number all issue notes; independent person reviews sequence regularly and follows up on missing/duplicate numbers
Head raw material storeman does NOT compare quality, quantity and description of physical raw materials to the requisition and transfer note Incorrect raw materials may be issued; raw materials can be stolen; financial losses due to incorrect production Storeman must compare QQD of physical goods to issue note; follow up differences; sign as evidence
Preparer of raw materials requisition does NOT sign as proof of preparation Responsibility cannot be allocated for fraud or error Preparer must sign the raw materials requisition as proof of preparation
No follow-up when production takes too long Production delays go undetected; resources wasted; deadlines missed Production schedule must include time estimates; production manager must regularly follow up on progress
No documents issued when finished goods are transferred to FG warehouse FG can be stolen in transit; inventory records for FG not updated accurately; production output cannot be verified Technician completes a pre-numbered FG transfer note (in duplicate); storeman in FG warehouse compares and signs; one copy remains in each location
No perpetual inventory records in component or FG warehouse Entity does not know available inventory at any time; theft/losses go undetected; cannot reconcile physical to records Implement a perpetual inventory system under a responsible officer (not the storeman); reconcile to general ledger and to physical count regularly; follow up differences

Goods Returned / Receiving Weaknesses (from S3.27)

WeaknessConsequenceRecommendation
Only one receipts clerk receives delivered goods (instead of two) Incorrect quantities/descriptions received without being detected; theft possible without witness Two receipts clerks must receive all delivered goods together

Inventory Count Procedures

A very popular exam topic — know the perfect system for Before / During / After the count

💡 From S3.27: Stock-take weakness questions follow the same WRC format. Common weaknesses: verbal instructions, same team counting their own area, no second team, no authorisation for changes to count sheets, no sequence check on count sheets, no instructions for damaged/obsolete goods.
  • Written instructions must be given to all counters and supervisors (not verbal)
  • Decide on date — year-end count as close to year-end as possible
  • Inform all parties involved early; arrange for enough staff
  • Warehouse must be packed neatly — similar items together, no open spaces
  • Count sheets must be pre-numbered; maintain a register of count sheets issued
  • Counters must complete count sheets in ink (no pencil, no Tippex)
  • Any changes must be authorised by the supervisor — supervisor recounts and initials
  • Allocate teams and supervisors — counters should NOT count their own area (no daily access)
  • Each warehouse area should be counted by two independent teams — differences followed up
  • Count all stock, including outside warehouses and warehouses elsewhere
  • Mark items as soon as counted (sticker) to prevent double counting
  • Limit movement of people during the count — only counters, supervisors, co-ordinator
  • Any inventory received or dispatched during the count must be kept separate and noted separately (cut-off)
  • Identify and exclude inventory belonging to third parties
  • Work systematically (start left, top of shelf, then floor)
  • After counting a section, team members compare results and follow up differences
  • Damaged, obsolete, slow-moving items must be identified and counted separately
  • Supervisor receives ALL count sheets back — checks that all are returned (sequence check)
  • Supervisor checks for unauthorised changes — all changes must be initialled
  • Both supervisors and co-ordinator should check number sequencing of count sheets (identify missing/duplicate)
  • Co-ordinator compares two teams' count sheets — follows up differences
  • Count results reconciled to perpetual inventory records — differences investigated
  • Reasons for differences identified: theft, recording errors, etc.
  • Perpetual records adjusted after authorisation if found to be incorrect
  • Damaged/obsolete items valued separately — do not overstate inventory value

Top Stock-Count Weaknesses from S3.27 (exam-tested)

  • Only verbal instructions given → counters may forget and count incorrectly → give written instructions
  • Team G counts their own daily area → fraud undetected → another team with protective gear should count area 4
  • Only one team counts area 4 → errors/fraud undetected → allocate a second independent team
  • No authorisation of changes on count sheets → fraudulent changes go undetected → supervisor must recount and sign every change
  • No sequence check of count sheets → count sheets can be lost or duplicated → supervisors AND co-ordinator check numbering
  • No instructions to look for damaged/obsolete goods → inventory overvalued → written instructions to identify and separate such items
