Auditing A2S1 – Salaries & Wages Cycle

Interactive Study Guide · Exam Friday

💼 Exam Prep

Overview

The Salaries & Wages Cycle — what makes it unique and how it's examined

📌 What makes this cycle special: It is an internally generated transaction — there is no external document to trigger payment. This makes it high risk for fictitious employees and fraud. The cycle has two sub-cycles: Wages (hourly workers, clock cards, paid weekly in cash) and Salaries (fixed monthly amount, paid by EFT).

Why is this cycle high-risk?

  • Material expense — wages affect inventory valuation (IAS 2); salaries are a significant cost
  • Impacts cash directly — large cash payouts create theft and fraud risk
  • Internally generated — no purchase order or external invoice; the company creates its own records
  • High volume of transactions — many employees, many periods, many possible errors
  • Fictitious employees — the biggest fraud risk: ghost employees created to siphon wages

V · A · C Applied to This Cycle

  • Validity Payments only to real, properly appointed employees · only for hours actually worked · at authorised tariffs · deductions properly authorised · dismissals authorised
  • Accuracy Correct hours × correct tariff = correct gross wage · correct deductions · correct net pay · correct classification (wages vs salaries vs expense)
  • Completeness All employees are paid · all wage transactions recorded · all deductions paid to third parties · no omissions

The 5 Stages of the Cycle

  • 1. Appointments & Authorisation — hiring, contracts, employee files
  • 2. Timekeeping (Wages only) — clock cards, supervision, hours worked
  • 3. Calculation & Preparation of Payroll — wages journal / salary journal
  • 4. Payment — wage envelopes (wages) or EFT (salaries) · unclaimed wages
  • 5. Deductions to Third Parties — SARS (PAYE), UIF, pension, medical aid

Salaries vs Wages

Know the key differences — the exam often tests both and the controls differ significantly

Feature 🧾 Wages 📋 Salaries
Basis of pay Paid for productive hours worked (hourly rate) Fixed pre-determined monthly amount (basic salary)
Frequency Paid weekly Paid monthly
Payment method Cash (wage envelopes) EFT (electronic transfer to bank account)
Timekeeping Clock cards required — time worked must be recorded and authorised No clock cards — hours do not affect pay
Key document Clock card → Wages Journal Appointment letter / Tariff form → Salary Journal
Calculation Hours worked × Tariff per hour − Deductions = Net Wage Basic Salary + Allowances/Fringe Benefits − Deductions = Net Salary
Unclaimed Unclaimed wages possible — must be recorded in unclaimed wage register and locked in safe No unclaimed wages — EFT goes directly to bank account
Fraud risk Ghost employees, buddy clocking (clocking in for others), inflated hours Ghost employees, fictitious salary increases, unauthorised allowances
💡 Calculation formula to memorise:
Wages: Hours worked (clock card) × Tariff (appointment letter / tariff amendment form) = Gross Wage − Deductions (deduction authorisation form) = Net Wage

Salaries: Basic Salary (appointment letter / tariff form) + Allowances / Fringe Benefits − Deductions (deduction authorisation form) = Net Salary

Documents & Flow

Know every document, who prepares it, its purpose, and how many copies

Appointment & Personnel Records

Appointment Contract

  • Prepared by HR after candidate is selected
  • Contains: appointment conditions, compensation, fringe benefits, deductions
  • Signed by HR Manager AND employee
  • 2 copies: employee + HR file
  • Triggers creation of employee file and deduction authorisation form

Employee File (Permanent File)

  • Created for every appointed employee
  • Contains: appointment letter, employee number, appointment date, compensation, fringe benefits, deductions
  • Maintained by HR — independent of payroll
  • Used to verify details in wages/salary journal

Deduction Authorisation Form

  • Authorises non-statutory deductions (e.g. pension, medical aid)
  • Signed by employee AND HR Manager
  • Required by law before including deductions in contract
  • 2 copies: employee + HR file

Tariff / Scale Amendment Form

  • Used when an employee's tariff/salary rate changes
  • Signed by employee AND line manager AND HR manager
  • Filed in employee file — used to verify tariff in wages journal

Timekeeping (Wages Only)

Clock Card (Time Card)

  • Pre-numbered and pre-printed — kept in a safe
  • Contains: employee name, employee number, date of the week
  • Issued Monday by admin clerk — employee must show staff card to receive it; admin clerk signs register
  • Employee clocks in/out using clock machine at the factory entrance — machine prints times automatically
  • At end of week: admin clerk calculates and records normal + overtime hours; supervisor/foreman authorises by signing
  • Returned to payroll clerk — a clock card register tracks issue and return
  • Sequence check performed — missing or duplicate cards investigated

Payroll Documents

Wages Journal (weekly)

  • Prepared by Payroll Clerk A
  • Contains: employee number, name, hours worked, tariff, gross wage, deductions, net wage, total wages for week
  • Reviewed and signed by Payroll Clerk B (independent)
  • Authorised by Payroll Manager (sample checks + budget comparison)
  • Signed by Financial Manager before payment

Salary Journal (monthly)

  • Prepared by Payroll Clerk A
  • Contains: employee number, name, date, scale/grade, allowances, deductions, gross & net salary
  • Reviewed by Payroll Clerk B (independent)
  • Authorised by Payroll Manager
  • Used as basis for EFT payment requisition

Other Key Documents

  • Wage Slip / Pay Slip — given to each employee (2 copies: employee + payroll); shows transactions per employee + year-to-date totals
  • Wage Pay-out Register — employees sign when collecting their wage envelope
  • Unclaimed Wage Register — records all wage envelopes not collected at pay-out
  • IRP5 Form — annual document per employee showing total payments received and tax deducted; used to pay PAYE to SARS
  • EFT Payment Requisition — triggers electronic salary payments; prepared by Payroll Clerk B, reviewed by Payroll Manager, authorised by Financial Manager

The Perfect System

What the ideal wages/salaries system looks like — compare this to the scenario in the question

  • Line manager submits a motivation for appointment to HR with job specifications — HR approves the need for the appointment (considering budget)
  • Advertisement placed internally and externally — applications reviewed by HR against job specs
  • At least two people (line manager + HR manager) conduct the interview
  • Shortlist compiled and reviewed by an independent second person
  • Before finalising: follow up references, check criminal record, verify qualifications, check aptitude/competency test results, copy ID, obtain proof of address and bank details
  • Appointment contract signed by HR Manager AND employee — 2 copies
  • Employee file (permanent record) created for every employee — maintained by HR (independent of payroll)
  • Deduction authorisation form completed and signed by employee and HR manager
  • Termination of service form prepared when employee leaves — signed by line manager and HR manager — prevents ghost employees
  • Clock cards are pre-numbered, pre-printed and stored in a safe
  • Admin Clerk A issues clock cards on Monday — employee must show staff card as identification; admin clerk signs a clock card register
  • Employee signs the clock card register as proof of receipt
  • Clock machine located at the entrance of the workspace — increases visibility
  • Foreman is present during clock-in and clock-out to supervise — ensures employees clock themselves (not others)
  • When overtime is worked: foreman remains on duty until the last employee leaves
  • At end of week: Admin Clerk B (different from A) calculates normal and overtime hours and records on clock card
  • Foreman / supervisor authorises clock cards by signing — specifically approves overtime
  • Admin Clerk A reconciles clock cards returned to the number issued per the register — follows up missing cards
  • Employee signs the register when returning the clock card
  • Independent person checks the number sequence of clock cards and investigates missing or duplicate numbers
  • Payroll Clerk A prepares the wages/salary journal — obtains hours from approved clock cards, tariff from appointment letter/tariff form, deductions from deduction authorisation form
  • Payroll Clerk B (independent review) checks:
    • Hours to clock cards
    • Tariff to appointment letter or tariff amendment form
    • Deductions to deduction authorisation form
    • Number of employees agrees to updated employee list from HR
    • Recalculates all calculations and signs as evidence
  • Payroll Manager authorises — performs above checks on a sample, compares total to budget, investigates differences or unusual items, signs journal
  • Financial Manager reviews details of wage/salary journal, recalculates, signs before payment is released
  • Reconcile current week/month's payroll to the prior period — follow up on differences (new employees, terminations, changes)

Preparation of wage envelopes:

  • Two payroll clerks independent of the wage journal process make up wage envelopes based on the approved wage journal
  • Each checks the other's work (independent review)
  • Wage envelopes are locked away in a safe until pay-out

Safekeeping and custody:

  • Line manager/department supervisor compares number of envelopes to list of employees and signs wage journal if satisfied

Wage pay-out:

  • Wage envelopes handed out by two independent people (line manager/supervisor + another independent person)
  • Upon collection, employee must:
    • Identify themselves (show staff card/ID)
    • Count money in envelope and compare to wage slip
    • Sign the wage pay-out register as proof of receipt
  • Employee is not allowed to collect an envelope on behalf of someone else
  • Both payout officials sign the wage pay-out register at the end of the pay-out

Unclaimed wages:

  • Line manager records unclaimed envelopes in the unclaimed wage register
  • Payroll secretary takes custody and stores in a safe; signs register as evidence of custody
  • When employee collects: identify themselves, count money, compare to wage slip and register, sign register
  • If not collected within a reasonable period: deposit back into business bank account using unclaimed wage register as supporting document
  • Independent manager follows up on long-outstanding unclaimed wages and employees who regularly appear on unclaimed wage register
  • Before releasing payment, verify: employee banking details against personnel records; payment from designated salary bank account; number of payments agrees to employee list; amounts agree to pay slips and salary journal
  • Payroll Clerk B loads EFT on internet banking
  • Payroll Manager (Approver A) — reviews and approves salary journal, then loads payment
  • Financial Manager (Approver B) — reviews salary journal, releases/authorises the EFT payment, and stamps wage journal as "PAID/CANCELLED"
  • Salary journal entries posted to general ledger; monthly bank reconciliation performed

Weaknesses, Consequences & Recommendations

From S4.30 (Ceramics) and your study material — the most exam-tested weaknesses per stage

📌 Exam structure: WRC questions are split by stage — Timekeeping / Calculation & Preparation / Wage Pay-out. Each row = 3 marks. Consequences often relate to fictitious employees, ghost payments, buddy clocking, theft, or inability to allocate responsibility.

Timekeeping Weaknesses

WeaknessConsequenceRecommendation
No supervision during clock-in and clock-out process Employees can clock in/out for other employees (buddy clocking) — particularly when foreman is not present at the entry/exit point Foreman must be present during all clock-in and clock-out processes AND must remain on duty during overtime until the last employee leaves
No review of the number sequence of clock cards AND no clock card register used for issue and return Duplicate clock cards may exist and go undetected; clock cards can go missing; not all clock cards accounted for Foreman (or independent person) must review number sequence of clock cards at end of each week and follow up missing/duplicate numbers AND a clock card register must be used to record issue and return of all clock cards
Blank clock cards are not stored securely (e.g. left in a box on the secretary's desk) Blank clock cards can be stolen and used to create fictitious employees; ghost workers can be added to payroll All pre-numbered clock cards must be kept in a locked safe until issued; access restricted to the responsible admin clerk
Employees collect their own clock cards without showing identification Employees can take clock cards for other (possibly fictitious) employees; responsibility cannot be allocated Admin clerk must only hand clock cards to employees personally upon presentation of their staff card; admin clerk signs the clock card register as evidence
Admin clerk calculates hours AND the foreman approves hours worked — same foreman also approves the wage journal Insufficient SoD — foreman can create fictitious hours in timekeeping and then approve them in the wages journal without independent check The two functions must be separated: one person approves normal/overtime hours; a DIFFERENT independent person approves the wage journal

Calculation & Preparation Weaknesses

WeaknessConsequenceRecommendation
No independent review/checking of the wage journal calculations Incorrect calculations go undetected; employees paid wrong amounts; deliberate manipulation not caught Payroll Clerk B (independent of Clerk A) must check hours against clock cards, tariff against appointment letter/tariff form, deductions against authorisation form, and recalculate — sign as evidence
Number of employees in wage journal is NOT agreed to the updated employee list from HR Fictitious (ghost) employees can be added to the payroll without detection Payroll Clerk B must agree the number of employees in the wages journal to the current, updated employee list maintained by HR
No reconciliation of current period wages to the prior period Unusual increases, new fictitious employees, or terminated employees still being paid are not detected Payroll Clerk B (or senior payroll clerk) must reconcile number of employees, hours, tariffs, and deductions to the prior week/month and follow up on any differences
Payroll manager does not compare total wages to budget before authorising Overpayments, fictitious employees, or unauthorised increases are not detected at management level Payroll manager must compare total wages/salaries to the budget and investigate any differences or unusual items before signing the journal
Increases are not approved in writing (no tariff amendment form completed) Unauthorised or incorrect increases can be granted; employees can be paid the wrong amount; disputes cannot be resolved All tariff/salary increases must be documented on a tariff amendment form, signed by the employee, line manager, and HR manager, and filed in the employee's permanent file

Wage Pay-out Weaknesses (S4.30 — Ceramics)

WeaknessConsequenceRecommendation
Sealed wage envelopes are stored in a cupboard (NOT a locked safe) in the accounting department One or more wage envelopes can be stolen before the pay-out Sealed wage envelopes must be locked away in a safe until the pay-out takes place
No identification of employees takes place when wages are paid out Wages can be paid to non-employees; wages can be stolen if one employee collects for another Employees must identify themselves (show staff card or ID) before receiving their wage envelope; wages must be paid to employees in person only — collection on behalf of someone else is not permitted
Employee does NOT check the amount in the wage envelope at pay-out AND does NOT sign for receipt Errors in wage envelopes go undetected; no proof that each employee personally received their wage; responsibility cannot be allocated Employee must open the envelope, count the money, and compare it to the wage slip; employee must sign next to their name on the wage pay-out register as proof of receipt
Insufficient SoD — same person(s) involved in both preparing wage envelopes AND making up the wage journal Manipulation of amounts in wage envelopes possible without detection; fictitious wages can be paid out Two payroll clerks independent of the wage journal process must make up wage envelopes; they check each other's work; the wage journal preparer must not have access to the physical cash

Control Objectives

Always start with "To ensure that…" — no people, no actions, no documents. Focus on V · A · C

🔑 From S4.30: When asked for validity only objectives, focus on: authorisation of appointments, actual hours worked, authorised tariffs, authorised deductions, authorised dismissals, and real (non-fictitious) employees.
  • To ensure that payment of wages/salaries occurs only to employees whose appointments were properly authorised. (1)
  • To ensure that payments of wages are only made for hours actually worked. (1)
  • To ensure that payment of wages/salaries takes place at authorised tariffs/rates. (1)
  • To ensure that deductions and fringe benefits/allowances are properly authorised. (1)
  • To ensure that dismissals are duly authorised. (1)
  • To ensure that all payments to employees are made to actual employees of the company (no fictitious employees). (1)
  • To ensure that overtime worked is authorised in accordance with company policy. (1)
  • To ensure that wages are calculated at the correct hourly rate and for the correct number of hours worked.
  • To ensure that all deductions are calculated correctly and at the correct amounts.
  • To ensure that net wages/salaries are calculated correctly (correct gross less correct deductions).
  • To ensure that wage/salary transactions are recorded at the correct amounts in the wages/salary journal.
  • To ensure that wage expenses are correctly classified in the financial statements.
  • To ensure that all employees are included in the wages/salary journal and that none are omitted.
  • To ensure that all wage/salary transactions are recorded in the correct period.
  • To ensure that all deductions are paid over to the relevant third parties (SARS, UIF, pension, medical aid) timeously.
  • To ensure that no wage/salary transactions are omitted from the accounting records.

⚠️ Common Exam Mistakes

  • Writing "The payroll clerk must verify hours against clock cards" — this is a control, not an objective. Remove the person, the action, and the document.
  • Forgetting to add "in terms of company policy" for validity objectives about authorisation
  • Confusing accuracy (correct amounts) with completeness (all transactions recorded) — they are different marks
  • Writing objectives for accuracy when the question only asks for validity — read the question carefully!
